Geophysical Methods for Arctic Mineral Exploration

By Dr. Anna Björk · 2026-03-23

geophysicsexplorationmagneticelectromagneticarctic

Geophysical surveys are a critical tool in mineral exploration, allowing geologists to "see" beneath the surface before drilling. In Arctic environments, geophysics faces unique challenges but also offers unique opportunities due to the characteristics of frozen ground and exposed bedrock.

Why Geophysics Matters

Geophysical surveys measure physical properties of the Earth (magnetism, density, electrical conductivity, radioactivity) to identify buried geological features that may indicate mineralization. Geophysics is used to:

  • **Identify buried intrusions**: Mafic and ultramafic intrusions (like Skaergaard) have distinct magnetic signatures
  • **Map geological structures**: Faults, shear zones, and rock contacts affect geophysical responses
  • **Detect sulfide mineralization**: Massive sulfide deposits are electrically conductive and can be detected by electromagnetic surveys
  • **Guide drilling**: Geophysical targets prioritize drill hole locations, reducing exploration risk and cost

    • Magnetic Surveys

    How it works: Measures variations in the Earth's magnetic field caused by differences in rock magnetism. Magnetic minerals (magnetite, pyrrhotite) create strong anomalies.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Frozen lakes and sea ice provide stable platforms for ground magnetic surveys during winter
  • Glacial cover can obscure bedrock magnetic signatures
  • Permafrost does not significantly affect magnetic measurements
  • Aeromagnetic surveys are the primary method for regional exploration in Greenland

    • Applications for Skaergaard:

  • The Skaergaard intrusion has a strong magnetic signature due to its iron-rich composition
  • Magnetic surveys can map the extent of the intrusion and identify internal layering
  • Magnetic gradients can indicate areas of higher sulfide mineralization

    • Electromagnetic Surveys

    How it works: Measures the electrical conductivity of subsurface rocks. Sulfide mineralization (especially massive sulfides) is highly conductive and creates detectable anomalies.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Frozen ground can increase electrical resistivity, affecting survey results
  • Permafrost layers can mask deeper conductive targets
  • Winter surveys may provide better results when ground is frozen and uniform
  • Ground EM surveys are limited by terrain; airborne EM (AEM) is preferred for regional surveys

    • Applications: EM surveys are particularly useful for detecting:

  • Massive sulfide deposits (zinc, lead, copper, gold)
  • Graphite deposits (highly conductive)
  • Structural conductors (faults, shear zones)

    • Gravity Surveys

    How it works: Measures variations in the Earth's gravitational field caused by differences in rock density. Dense rocks (mafic intrusions, massive sulfides) create positive gravity anomalies.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Gravity measurements are less affected by surface conditions than EM surveys
  • Ice cover provides a stable platform for winter gravity surveys
  • GPS accuracy at high latitudes is generally good (unlike near the poles)

    • Applications for Skaergaard:

  • Gravity surveys can delineate the shape and volume of the Skaergaard intrusion
  • Density variations within the intrusion may indicate mineralized horizons
  • Gravity data complements magnetic data for comprehensive intrusion mapping

    • Induced Polarization (IP)

    How it works: Measures the ability of subsurface rocks to store and release electrical charge. Sulfide mineralization and clay alteration create IP anomalies.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Frozen ground increases resistivity, which can make IP surveys more challenging
  • Winter surveys may provide more consistent results due to uniform ground conditions
  • Ground IP requires electrode contact, which can be difficult in frozen or rocky terrain

    • Applications:

  • IP is one of the most effective methods for detecting disseminated sulfide mineralization (the style found at Skaergaard)
  • IP chargeability anomalies can directly target PGM-bearing sulfide zones

    • Radiometric Surveys

    How it works: Measures natural gamma radiation from potassium, uranium, and thorium in surface rocks. Different rock types have distinct radiometric signatures.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Radiometric surveys are typically airborne and are not significantly affected by Arctic conditions
  • Snow cover can attenuate surface radiation signals
  • Best conducted during snow-free periods

    • Applications:

  • Mapping surface geology and rock types
  • Identifying alteration zones associated with mineralization
  • Regional geological mapping in poorly exposed terrain

    • Seismic Surveys

    How it works: Measures the reflection of seismic waves from subsurface geological boundaries. Commonly used in oil and gas exploration.

    Arctic considerations:

  • Permafrost creates a complex seismic velocity structure
  • Frozen ground provides good coupling for seismic sources
  • Sea ice can provide a platform for marine seismic surveys

    • Applications in mining:

  • Less commonly used in mineral exploration than in oil and gas
  • Can provide depth-to-bedrock estimates in glacial-covered terrain
  • May become more important as exploration targets deeper targets

    • Remote Sensing

    How it works: Satellite and airborne imagery to identify surface features, alteration zones, and geological structures.

    Arctic applications:

  • Multispectral and hyperspectral imagery can identify alteration zones associated with mineralization
  • Radar imagery can penetrate cloud cover (common in the Arctic) and map surface features
  • Digital elevation models (DEM) from satellite data support geological mapping and infrastructure planning

    • Cost Comparison

  • **Airborne magnetic/radiometric**: US$50-150 per line kilometer
  • **Airborne EM**: US$100-300 per line kilometer
  • **Ground gravity**: US$5-15 per station
  • **Ground IP/resistivity**: US$10-30 per station
  • **Remote sensing**: US$5-50 per square kilometer (varies by resolution)

    • A typical Arctic regional exploration program (magnetics, EM, gravity, radiometrics) costs US$200K-500K.

    Conclusion

    Geophysical surveys are essential for Arctic mineral exploration, providing subsurface information that guides drilling and reduces exploration risk. The frozen ground, ice cover, and exposed bedrock of Arctic environments create both challenges and opportunities for geophysical surveys. For Skaergaard and other Greenland projects, geophysics has played a critical role in defining targets and will continue to be important as exploration extends to new areas.
