Structural Traps for Fluid Flow: Where Mineralization Actually Gets Stuck

By Dr. Anna Björk · 2026-03-23

Mineralization Is a Geography of Motion

Sulfides and PGMs do not spread uniformly. They concentrate where fluid pathways lose velocity, where chemistry changes, and where structures mechanically focus flow.

In Greenland’s orogenic belts, mineralization is often linked to structures that act as traps. These traps can be:

  • intersections of faults and folds
  • shear zone segments where permeability changes
  • lithologic boundaries where rheology shifts

    • From an exploration standpoint, the most important aspect is that traps create geometry. Geometry creates intercept plans. Intercept plans decide whether a hole yields usable mineralization data or just an expensive “maybe.”

    Turning Structure into a Drill-Azimuth Problem

    Teams often speak about drill direction as if it is a detail. In structural trap environments, it is central.

    A practical trap model has three measurable components:

  • **Trend**: the strike direction of the corridor
  • **Dip**: how steeply the trap plunges
  • **Continuity**: whether the trap is segmented and how far segments can be traced

    • If your geophysics suggests a conductive lineament but structural mapping suggests the trap is narrower than the conductor, you adjust azimuth and hole trajectory to maximize the probability of sampling the actual trapped zone.

    This is why exploration programs that integrate structural geology with geophysics tend to learn faster.

    Drilling for Trap Validation: The Two-Hole Philosophy

    A common early-stage strategy is to drill two holes that bracket the interpreted trap geometry.

  • Hole A tests the assumed trap plane.
  • Hole B tests the alternative dip or segment boundary.

    • If both holes show mineralized intervals in the expected relationship, the model strengthens rapidly. If one hole shows the corridor but not the trap, the data still matters. It tells you which part of the system is real and which is model overreach.

    This staged validation reduces the risk of wide spacing drilling that can create ambiguous results.

    Core Logging as Structural Evidence

    Structural traps are not just something you map on surface. In drilling, you should treat core logging as structural measurement.

    That typically means:

  • recording fracture sets and their orientation
  • logging shear textures and alteration boundaries
  • noting whether mineralization is vein-like, disseminated, or structurally controlled

    • If you combine that structural evidence with geophysics interpretation, the next drill round becomes more precise.

    Linking to Permitting: Staged Work That Minimizes Disturbance

    From an EIA perspective, staged work has a defensible logic: you reduce uncertainty before expanding the footprint.

    A trap-validation drilling philosophy supports this because it aims to:

  • use the minimum number of validation holes
  • avoid unnecessary deep drilling on low-confidence geometries
  • focus field activities on the most information-dense targets

    • Regulators and EIA teams tend to respond well to programs that demonstrate a “learn fast, then scale” approach rather than repeated expansions without model updates.

    Implications for Skaergaard EIA Momentum

    Skaergaard’s position as Greenland’s most advanced PGM narrative depends on credibility. Credibility in exploration is a product of disciplined iteration.

    Even when a project is pre-production, the underlying exploration mindset still matters for:

  • refining the geological model for resource confidence
  • ensuring that environmental and engineering planning is grounded in real rock response

    • Trap-based drilling and logging is the type of workflow that makes a big project feel “engineerable.”

    Bottom Line

    Structural traps are where mineralization “gets stuck.”

    If your drill program is designed to validate those traps geometrically, and you let core logging measure the trap, you increase your chance of both geological success and a smoother permitting narrative.
