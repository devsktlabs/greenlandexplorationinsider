How Mineral Deposits Are Formed: A Layered Intrusion Special

By Dr. Anna Björk · 2026-03-23

geologylayered intrusionsmineral depositsskaergaard

The Skaergaard deposit in East Greenland is one of the world's most geologically significant mineral deposits. Understanding how it formed is key to understanding why it is so valuable and how it compares to other PGM deposits globally. This article explains the science behind layered intrusion mineral deposits.

What Is a Layered Intrusion?

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

A layered intrusion is a large body of magma that cooled slowly beneath the Earth's surface, forming distinct horizontal layers of different rock types. These layers are created by a process called fractional crystallization:

1. Magma intrusion: Hot, molten rock (magma) is injected into the Earth's crust from below
2. Slow cooling: The magma cools slowly over thousands of years (vs rapid cooling at a volcano)
3. Crystal settling: As the magma cools, different minerals crystallize at different temperatures. Dense minerals (rich in iron, PGMs, and gold) settle to the bottom, while lighter minerals float to the top
4. Layer formation: This repeated process creates distinct layers of different rock types, called cumulate layers

The Layering Process

Think of the magma chamber as a giant pot of cooling soup. Just as heavier ingredients settle to the bottom while lighter ones float, minerals settle in the magma chamber:

Bottom layers (crystallized first, at highest temperatures):

  • Dense, iron-rich minerals (chromite, magnetite)
  • PGMs (palladium, platinum, rhodium) are concentrated here
  • Gold may also be concentrated in these layers
  • This is where the most valuable mineralization occurs at Skaergaard

Middle layers (crystallized at intermediate temperatures):

  • Olivine, pyroxene, and plagioclase (common rock-forming minerals)
  • Lower PGM and gold concentrations
  • This is the "bulk" of the intrusion

Top layers (crystallized last, at lowest temperatures):

  • Lighter, silica-rich minerals (granite-like compositions)
  • Generally lower metal concentrations
  • May contain some late-stage mineralization

Why Layered Intrusions Host PGM Deposits

PGMs (platinum group metals) are siderophile elements: they preferentially bond with iron and other dense metals rather than remaining dissolved in the magma. This behavior causes PGMs to:

  • Concentrate in early-forming minerals: PGMs crystallize early and settle with dense iron-rich minerals
  • Be carried by sulfide liquids: PGMs are chalcophile (sulfur-loving) and readily dissolve in immiscible sulfide liquids that form in the magma. These sulfide liquids are denser than the magma and sink, carrying PGMs to the bottom of the chamber
  • Form economic concentrations: The repeated settling and concentration process creates PGM grades high enough to be economically mined

The Sulfide Saturation Factor

A critical factor in PGM deposit formation is sulfide saturation. Sulfide saturation occurs when the magma can no longer hold all of its dissolved sulfur, and a separate sulfide liquid forms:

  • Before sulfide saturation: PGMs remain dissolved in the magma and are dispersed (uneconomic)
  • At sulfide saturation: A dense sulfide liquid forms, scavenging PGMs and other metals from the magma. This sulfide liquid sinks, concentrating metals at the base of the chamber
  • The R factor: The ratio of silicate magma to sulfide liquid determines how efficiently PGMs are concentrated. Higher R factors produce higher-grade PGM mineralization

The Skaergaard Intrusion Specifically

Skaergaard is one of the world's most-studied layered intrusions:

  • Age: Approximately 55 million years old (Tertiary period)
  • Size: Approximately 8 kilometers wide and 4 kilometers thick at the thickest point
  • Composition: Primarily gabbro (a dark, coarse-grained igneous rock)
  • Formation: Formed during the opening of the North Atlantic, when massive volcanic activity created numerous intrusions along the East Greenland margin

Skaergaard's mineralization:

  • PGMs and gold are concentrated in specific layers near the base of the intrusion
  • The mineralized layers are relatively flat-lying and extend across a large area
  • Gold is particularly abundant at Skaergaard compared to other layered intrusions, giving it its unique dual PGM-gold profile

Other Famous Layered Intrusions

Understanding Skaergaard's context requires comparing it to other layered intrusions:

Bushveld Complex (South Africa):

  • The world's largest layered intrusion and the source of approximately 80% of the world's PGMs
  • Approximately 2 billion years old, 400 kilometers in diameter
  • Multiple PGM-bearing layers (Merensky Reef, UG2 chromitite, Platreef)
  • Very different age and tectonic setting from Skaergaard but similar mineralization processes

Stillwater Complex (Montana, USA):

  • Approximately 2.7 billion years old
  • Hosts the J-M Reef, one of the world's richest PGM horizons
  • Currently mined by Sibanye-Stillwater

Great Dyke (Zimbabwe):

  • Approximately 2.6 billion years old
  • Hosts PGM-bearing sulfide layers (Main Sulfide Zone)
  • Significant PGM producer in Africa

What Layered Intrusion Geology Tells Investors

For investors evaluating layered intrusion PGM deposits:

  • Predictability: Layered intrusions have relatively predictable geology. Once the layering pattern is understood, mineralization can be projected laterally and at depth with reasonable confidence
  • Scale: Layered intrusions are large geological features. Economic mineralization often extends across large areas, supporting long mine lives
  • Multiple horizons: Some intrusions have multiple mineralized horizons (like the Bushveld's Merensky and UG2). Each horizon provides additional resource
  • Grade continuity: Layered intrusion mineralization typically shows good grade continuity along the layer strike, which is favorable for resource estimation

Exploration Implications

Layered intrusion exploration follows a systematic approach:

1. Geological mapping: Identify the intrusion and map the layering pattern
2. Geophysics: Use magnetic and gravity surveys to delineate the intrusion's shape and depth
3. Geochemistry: Surface sampling and analysis to identify PGM-bearing layers
4. Diamond drilling: Drill test the most prospective layers to define resources

Conclusion

Layered intrusions are geological systems that naturally concentrate PGMs and other valuable metals through fractional crystallization and sulfide segregation. Skaergaard is a world-class example of this process, with unique gold enrichment that distinguishes it from other layered intrusion deposits. For investors, understanding the geological process behind layered intrusion deposits provides confidence in the predictability, scale, and continuity of the mineralization at Skaergaard.
