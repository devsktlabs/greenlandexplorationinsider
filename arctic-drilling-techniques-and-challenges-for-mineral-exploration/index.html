Arctic Drilling: Techniques and Challenges for Mineral Exploration

By Dr. Anna Björk · 2026-03-23

arctic drillingexplorationdiamond drillingpermafrost

Diamond drilling is the primary method for defining mineral resources in Arctic environments. The extreme cold, permafrost, and remote location create challenges that affect cost, schedule, and data quality. This article explains the techniques and considerations for Arctic drilling.

Diamond Drilling Basics

Diamond drilling uses a diamond-encrusted drill bit to cut a cylindrical core from the rock. The core provides direct geological information:

  • **Core diameter**: Commonly NQ (47.6mm), HQ (63.5mm), or PQ (85mm). Larger diameters provide more geological information but cost more per meter
  • **Recovery rate**: The percentage of the drilled interval recovered as core. High recovery (90%+) is essential for reliable resource estimation
  • **Drill depth**: Arctic diamond rigs can typically drill to 500-1,500 meters depth, depending on terrain and conditions

    • Arctic-Specific Challenges

    Frozen ground and permafrost:

  • Permafrost can extend 200-600 meters below surface in East Greenland
  • Drilling through frozen ground creates challenges for core recovery and borehole stability
  • Frozen formation water can freeze around the drill string, causing stuck pipe
  • Mitigation: Heated drilling fluid, anti-freeze additives, and specialized casing programs

    • Water supply:

  • Diamond drilling requires water for circulation (drilling fluid) and core washing
  • Arctic water sources may be frozen or distant from drill sites
  • Mitigation: Water wells with heaters, water delivery by snowmobile or helicopter, closed-circuit fluid systems

    • Ice and snow:

  • Drill platforms must be stable on ice and snow
  • Snow accumulation can bury equipment and create access problems
  • Mitigation: Portable drill platforms, heated drill shelters, regular snow removal

    • Logistics:

  • Drill rigs, fuel, water, and supplies must be transported to remote Arctic locations by helicopter, sled, or boat
  • Mobilization and demobilization are expensive and time-consuming
  • Weather delays can significantly extend drilling schedules

    • Environmental sensitivity:

  • Drilling in pristine Arctic environments requires strict environmental protocols
  • All drilling fluids must be non-toxic and contained
  • Drill site rehabilitation is required after drilling is complete

    • Drilling Techniques

    Conventional diamond drilling:

  • Standard technique using a wireline system to retrieve core from the drill string
  • Most common method for mineral exploration in Arctic environments
  • Can be adapted for frozen conditions with heated drilling fluids

    • Reverse circulation (RC) drilling:

  • Uses compressed air to blow cuttings to the surface instead of recovering core
  • Faster and cheaper than diamond drilling but provides less geological information
  • Limited application in Arctic conditions due to air compression challenges in cold

    • Directional drilling:

  • Deviating drill holes from vertical to target specific mineralized zones
  • Useful for testing mineralization at depth from a single drill pad
  • More complex and expensive but reduces surface disturbance and mobilization costs

    • Seasonal Constraints

    Arctic drilling is primarily conducted during the field season:

    Summer drilling (June-September):

  • Ground may be thawed above the active layer, simplifying some drilling challenges
  • Helicopter access is available for remote sites
  • Longer daylight hours extend the working day (24-hour daylight at some locations)

    • Winter drilling (February-May):

  • Frozen ground and water surfaces enable access to areas that are swampy or inaccessible in summer
  • Ice roads can support heavy equipment transport
  • Shorter daylight hours and extreme cold create operational challenges
  • Drill shelters with heating are essential

    • Shoulder season (October-January):

  • Transitional conditions with unpredictable weather
  • Generally avoided for drilling operations

    • Core Logging and Sampling

    Drill core must be logged, sampled, and assayed:

  • **Core logging**: Detailed geological description of the core, including rock type, mineralization, alteration, and structural features
  • **Photography**: High-resolution photographs of the core for documentation
  • **Sampling**: Core is split in half, with one half retained as reference and the other half submitted for assay
  • **Assay**: Core samples are analyzed for metal content at a certified laboratory
  • **QA/QC**: Certified reference materials, blanks, and duplicates are inserted into the sample stream for quality control

    • In Arctic conditions, core logging is typically conducted in heated shelters or at a base camp to ensure worker comfort and data quality.

    Cost of Arctic Drilling

    Arctic drilling is significantly more expensive than drilling in temperate-zone locations:

  • **Mobilization**: US$50,000-200,000 per drill rig (helicopter transport to remote site)
  • **Drilling rate**: US$150-400 per meter (vs US$80-200/m in temperate zones)
  • **Daily camp cost**: US$5,000-15,000 per day for a remote Arctic exploration camp
  • **Total cost**: A 2,000-meter Arctic drilling program typically costs US$500K-1.5M

    • Skaergaard Drilling Program

    Skaergaard's resource definition has been supported by multiple drilling campaigns:

  • **Total drilling**: Thousands of meters of diamond drilling defining the Indicated resource
  • **Drill hole spacing**: Spacing sufficient for Indicated classification (typically 50-100 meter spacing)
  • **Next steps**: Infill drilling to upgrade portions of the resource to Measured category, and extension drilling to test potential depth and lateral extensions

    • Conclusion

    Arctic diamond drilling is technically challenging and expensive, but essential for defining mineral resources. Frozen ground, logistics, and environmental sensitivity add cost and complexity. For Skaergaard, the existing drilling program has defined a robust combined indicated and inferred resource, and additional drilling will upgrade portions to Measured category as the project advances toward feasibility. The cost of Arctic drilling is a necessary investment in resource confidence that underpins the project's economic value.
