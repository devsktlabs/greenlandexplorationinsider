Glacial Logistics: Building Drill Corridors Without Fighting the Arctic

By Dr. Anna Björk · 2026-03-23

[Greenlanddrilling logisticsfield seasonEIAcore recoveryexploration planning]

Logistics Is Part of the Geological Model

In many greenfield projects, logistics is treated like a background constraint. In Greenland, it is a driver of data quality.

A drill program is not only about the downhole physics. It is also about:

  • where the rig can be staged safely
  • how cuttings and fluids are managed
  • how quickly cores can be transported to maintain measurement integrity
  • how repeatable the sampling workflow is across changing weather windows

    • These practicalities shape the geology interpretation because they affect what you actually get out of the ground: recovery, structural orientation, contaminant risk, and even the ability to resurvey drill collars if required.

    Access Design: Think in Corridors, Not Points

    A geologist’s dream is the ideal drill pad. A field team’s dream is a corridor that works.

    A corridor-based approach typically includes:

  • **Pre-planned access routes** using the most stable ground options available in-season.
  • **Minimal branching**: every detour means more disturbance and more time.
  • **Clear staging areas** for the drill, equipment, and sampling/QA-logging.

    • This becomes directly relevant to EIA content. Permitting packages prefer a clear explanation of “why this footprint” and “why this sequence.” When access is rationalized as a corridor strategy, environmental impacts are easier to quantify and minimize.

    Seasonal Work Windows: Matching Geology Sampling to the Calendar

    Greenland’s field seasons are short, and weather volatility can compress timelines. A smart exploration plan uses staged objectives:

  • **Early season**: collaring and establishing core logistics. The goal is to lock in sampling quality and verify that the drill orientation and method intersect the intended structure.
  • **Mid season**: execute the most information-dense holes when conditions are most stable.
  • **Late season**: focus on confirmatory holes or reduce commitment to risky intervals.

    • This is not just scheduling. It is a quality-control strategy. If core logging becomes rushed late in the season, structural measurements and lithologic contacts lose resolution.

    Pad Minimization Improves Both EIA and Data Integrity

    There is a tempting trade-off between an oversized pad (easier rig operations) and a minimized pad (less disturbance). In practice, the best programs optimize for a middle ground:

  • smaller pads with better engineered surfaces reduce habitat disturbance
  • reduced heavy movement lowers the chance of equipment-related contamination of sampling streams
  • better-defined staging reduces the probability of sample mix-ups under time pressure

    • Even small improvements in sampling chain-of-custody can reduce costly retesting later.

    Core Recovery Bias: Planning for What the Rock Will Refuse

    Arctic bedrock can be fractured, chemically altered, or variably competent. That variability creates recovery bias.

    To manage it, field programs often adopt:

  • pre-drill assumptions about fracture density based on structural mapping and geophysics
  • a coring plan that anticipates where blocky or incompetent intervals might reduce recovery
  • logging protocols designed to record uncertainty explicitly

    • If you do not plan for recovery bias, you end up interpreting missing intervals as absence of mineralization. In high-grade-target environments, that is a fast path to underestimating potential.

    Feedback Loop: How Logistics Informs Interpretation

    A high-quality exploration program establishes a feedback loop between field execution and the geological model.

    After each hole, the team should quickly update:

  • whether the drill intersected the intended structure at the expected dip
  • whether core recovery and lithologic contact positions match predictions
  • whether the geophysical anomaly is behaving coherently at drill scale

    • If the first hole is off-structure, the logistics plan often needs revisiting too. Wrong assumptions can force additional re-entry or sidetracks, which increases footprint and can complicate EIA scheduling.

    Implications for Skaergaard-Scale Programs

    Skaergaard’s scale and importance means the exploration narrative must be credible and repeatable. Logistics discipline is part of that credibility.

    For a large project, the operational advantage is that you can standardize:

  • access routes and staging templates
  • core handling workflows
  • QA/QC checklists
  • communication protocols between drilling crew, geologists, and the EIA baseline team

    • Standardization reduces “unknown unknowns,” which investors like because it tightens timelines and reduces cost overruns.

    Bottom Line

    Drilling in Greenland is a coupled system: geology determines targets, but logistics determines data quality.

    If you design drill corridors to minimize disturbance while protecting core handling integrity, you improve both your EIA story and your subsurface interpretation.
