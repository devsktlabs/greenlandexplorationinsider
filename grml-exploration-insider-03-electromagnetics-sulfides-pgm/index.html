Why Conductivity Is Not a Commodity, It’s a Clue

In Greenland exploration, conductive signals are often treated as a proxy for sulfides. That is sometimes true, but not always. Conductivity can reflect:

  • sulfide-rich zones
  • graphite-bearing or altered lithologies
  • water-saturated fracture networks
  • man-made or cultural artifacts in accessible zones

    • If you treat every conductor as ore, you drill more holes than your budget can justify. If you treat conductors as pure noise, you can miss the one structural corridor that matters.

    The goal is to interpret conductivity as a clue that must be integrated with structure, lithology, and uncertainty.

    Building a Conductivity Narrative Before You Drill

    A strong PGM exploration narrative is usually built in layers.

    Layer 1: Regional mapping

    Start with airborne or regional geophysics to identify:

  • conductivity trends
  • magnetic lineaments that might suggest mafic associations or structural fabrics
  • structural corridors that can host fluid migration pathways

    • This stage should produce a set of “corridor hypotheses,” not a single target.

    Layer 2: Ground truth refinement

    Then refine the strongest corridors with ground-based methods.

    For Greenland, a common refinement path is:

  • electromagnetic profiling for conductive strength and geometry
  • induced polarization style methods (where available operationally) to help distinguish chargeability patterns associated with sulfides
  • targeted gravity or magnetic re-interpretation to improve lithology contrasts

    • Layer 3: Corridor-scale confidence

    Translate refined anomalies into drill-scale expectations:

  • expected dip directions
  • likely thickness zones
  • whether the anomaly should remain coherent at the depth range of interest

    • A drill plan that is honest about uncertainty can still be decisive. It simply needs to be decisive in the right direction.

    Drilling Decisions: Collar Placement and Intercept Strategy

    EM interpretation affects two practical drilling choices:

  • **Where to drill (collars):** collars should target the highest-confidence parts of the corridor, not just the widest anomaly.
  • **How to intercept:** if the conductor is suspected to be structure-controlled, you want hole trajectories that have the best chance to cut through the focused zone.

    • In field terms, this means you may choose a slightly less impressive conductivity peak if it offers a better intercept geometry based on structural interpretation.

    Sampling Priorities: Make the Core Do the Work

    Conductive geophysics tells you where to drill. Core and logging tell you what it means.

    A PGM-focused drilling team typically increases attention on:

  • sulfide abundance and texture
  • vein vs disseminated sulfide relationships
  • alteration mineralogy that might indicate evolving fluid conditions
  • structural orientation of mineralized intervals

    • The key is to pre-define what you will look for on logging day so that the first pass of data is usable for model update, not just descriptive.

    EIA-Friendly Field Sequencing

    Geophysics interpretation also affects permitting in a subtle way.

    If you sequence exploration so that each field activity reduces uncertainty before you scale up disturbance, you can frame your program as a staged, adaptive workflow.

    Example logic that tends to hold up:

  • run low-footprint geophysical work to define corridors
  • drill the smallest number of validation holes that can confirm geometry
  • scale to deeper or more extensive drilling only after model update

    • This reduces unnecessary drilling, which can shorten the overall disturbance footprint. Investors may not always see this directly, but EIA teams and regulators respond to it.

    Implications for Skaergaard-Style Projects

    Skaergaard’s scale and maturity mean there is an expectation that the early-stage modeling workflow is robust. For EM-based corridor targets in similar environments, the practical lesson is:

  • EM anomalies must be integrated with structural and lithologic constraints
  • drill intercept strategy must be planned around expected corridor thickness and dip
  • logging and sampling protocols must be designed to resolve ambiguity quickly

    • If those elements line up, conductivity becomes a useful guide to finding the right part of the ore system.

    Bottom Line

    Conductivity in Greenland is a clue, not an answer. The highest success programs treat EM as one component of a corridor narrative, design drill intercepts around that narrative, and use core logging to update confidence fast.
